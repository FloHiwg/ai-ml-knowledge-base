## What Is Continual Learning?

Training a model on a sequence of new tasks over time, without forgetting what it already knows. A human analogy: learning a new language shouldn't make you forget your native one.

**Stability-plasticity tradeoff:** Every model update that improves performance on a new task risks degrading performance on prior ones. The goal of continual learning is to navigate this tradeoff.

---

## Catastrophic Forgetting

When training on new data, gradient updates overwrite representations that encode prior knowledge. The more different (non-IID) the new data is from the original training distribution, the worse the forgetting.

**Metrics used in research:**
- `AvgAcc` — average accuracy across all tasks at end of sequential training
- `FM` (Forgetting Measure) — average drop from peak per-task accuracy (0 = no forgetting)
- Multi-task joint training = upper bound / ideal baseline

---

## Experimental Frameworks

| Framework | Description |
|---|---|
| **Batch-incremental** | Sequential exposure to whole task datasets; no past data available during each stage |
| **Domain adaptation** | Special case: one new domain, measure forgetting on prior capabilities |
| **Streaming learning** | Real-time updates, one or a few examples at a time |
| **Multi-task (joint)** | All data available simultaneously — ceiling performance |

Most LLM research uses batch-incremental or domain adaptation. True real-world continual learning is messier and more open-ended than either.

## Classic Mitigation Techniques

### Replay Buffers

Store a curated subset of prior data; mix it into new-task training. Simple and effective. Hard for LLMs: the original pretraining corpus is vast and often unavailable. Feasible for instruction-tuning data (more manageable volume).

### Knowledge Distillation

Combine new-task training loss with a distillation loss that penalises the model for drifting from its prior outputs. Keeps representations anchored to prior behaviour.

### Regularization

- **EWC (Elastic Weight Consolidation):** protect the most important parameters for prior tasks from large updates
- **KL divergence penalty:** directly penalise output distribution shift from a reference model (same mechanism as in RLHF/DPO)
- Lowering the learning rate also reduces forgetting

### Architectural

- Add new modules (e.g., LoRA adapters) per task, freezing prior ones
- Mixture-of-Experts architectures are inherently more resistant to forgetting

## RL vs SFT for Continual Learning

The central finding of recent research: **on-policy RL naturally resists catastrophic forgetting without any explicit continual learning mechanism**. SFT consistently causes significant forgetting.

### Empirical results (Lai et al. 2025)

Sequential training on 7 vision-QA tasks with no replay buffer:

| Method | AvgAcc | FM (forgetting) | General benchmarks |
|---|---|---|---|
| SFT | 54.0% | -10.4% | Degrades |
| GRPO (RL) | 60.0% | -2.3% | Maintained or improved |
| Multi-task upper bound | 62.9% | — | — |

### Why RL Forgets Less: Three Explanations

**1. Forward vs Reverse KL**

| | SFT | RL |
|---|---|---|
| Objective | Minimise negative log-likelihood (forward KL) | Maximise expected reward (reverse KL) |
| Behaviour | **Mode-covering** — must assign probability to every mode in the dataset | **Mode-seeking** — finds high-reward modes, can ignore others |
| Effect on forgetting | Spreads probability mass over new data, crushing unrepresented prior knowledge | Preserves prior modes that don't appear in reward signal |

**2. RL's Razor (Shenfeld et al. 2025)**

Among all solutions that achieve high reward on a new task, RL's on-policy updates are biased toward the one with **minimal KL divergence from the base model**:

> *"RL's Razor: on-policy methods are inherently biased toward solutions that remain closer to the original policy in KL divergence — minimising forgetting as a side effect."*

KL divergence between base and finetuned models on the target dataset is the **single most reliable predictor** of catastrophic forgetting — more predictive than gradient magnitude, sparsity, or rank.

**3. On-Policy Data Is the Root Cause**

The forgetting resistance is not from:
- KL regularization in the RL objective (removing it doesn't change forgetting)
- GRPO specifically (naive REINFORCE shows the same pattern)
- Long CoT / reasoning traces

It **is** from on-policy sampling:
- On-policy SFT (SFT on samples generated by the current policy) matches RL's forgetting resistance
- Iterative SFT (regenerate data each epoch) substantially reduces forgetting
- SimPO (offline preference tuning) resembles SFT forgetting behaviour

**Practical guideline:** If you can't run full RL, regenerate your SFT data from the current model at the start of each epoch.

## EAFT — Entropy-Adaptive Fine-Tuning

A drop-in modification to SFT that closes most of the forgetting gap without switching to RL.

**Diagnosis:** SFT data contains "Confident Conflicts" — tokens where the model is very confident (low entropy) but the supervised target conflicts with that confidence (low probability under the model). These cause large, destructive gradient updates.

**Fix:** Weight each token's cross-entropy loss by its normalised entropy:

```
EAFT_loss(token_t) = normalised_entropy(token_t) × CE_loss(token_t)
```

- Low-entropy tokens (confident conflicts) → near-zero weight → updates suppressed
- High-entropy tokens (genuine uncertainty) → full weight → learning proceeds normally
- Computed over Top-K=20 tokens only (minimal compute overhead)

**Results:** Validated on math (4B–32B models), medical, and tool-use domains. Matches RL's forgetting resistance while maintaining SFT's in-domain performance advantage.

## RL Generalizes; SFT Memorizes

Beyond just retaining old knowledge, RL also **improves out-of-distribution performance** while SFT hurts it:

| Method | OOD generalization |
|---|---|
| RL | +3–11% improvement |
| SFT | Up to -79.5% degradation |

**SFT → RL pipeline for compositional reasoning:**
1. SFT teaches atomic/foundational skills efficiently
2. RL synthesizes those skills into composite, generalizable reasoning

Either alone is suboptimal. Math-specific RL training generalizes to non-math tasks; math SFT does not. On-policy data and the presence of a negative gradient (penalizing incorrect responses) are the key contributors.

## The Missing Sleep Equivalent

Karpathy identifies a deeper form of continual learning that LLMs lack entirely — not just resistance to forgetting, but an active consolidation mechanism analogous to sleep:

- During sleep, humans distill context-window experience into long-term memory
- The process involves reviewing events, generating synthetic scenarios, and strengthening important patterns
- Every LLM session starts from scratch — no equivalent of overnight consolidation

A possible implementation: after each session, a distillation phase that takes the context window, analyzes it, generates synthetic data, and updates a small sparse subset of weights (e.g., a per-user LoRA). This would give each instance a genuine individual memory rather than just a shared pre-trained prior.

See [[concepts/agi-and-intelligence]] for the full framing of missing cognitive capabilities.

---

## Implications

- RL's natural resistance to forgetting is a direct consequence of its online nature — no architectural changes needed
- For LLM post-training pipelines, RL is not just better for alignment — it is also better for retaining general capabilities
- This has direct implications for building adaptive, generally intelligent systems: RL's properties align with the adaptability requirement of AGI
- EAFT provides a practical middle ground when RL is too expensive
- Replay buffers remain relevant for instruction-tuning scale (manageable data volumes)
